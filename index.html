📖 Background: The Historic Regulatory Change

For centuries, property ownership in Makkah and Madinah was restricted exclusively to Saudi nationals. The recent regulatory amendment allowing non-Saudi Muslims to own property within the Haram boundaries represents one of the most significant policy shifts in the history of Islamic real estate. This change was enacted under Vision 2030 with the explicit goal of deepening the spiritual connection between the global Muslim community and the two holy cities. The addressable market of 2 billion Muslims — spanning Indonesia, Pakistan, Bangladesh, India, Turkey, Egypt, Nigeria, and dozens of other nations — now has legal access to ownership in Islam's holiest city for the first time in modern history.

📊 Vision 2030 Alignment

Masar Makkah is a flagship component of Saudi Vision 2030's pilgrimage economy strategy. The Vision targets growing annual pilgrims from ~13M (pre-COVID baseline) to 30M by 2030, and 150M Umrah visitors annually. This is underpinned by SAR 1 trillion+ in tourism infrastructure investment, including NEOM, the Red Sea Project, and the Haramain High Speed Railway. Masar sits at the nexus of all these initiatives — the infrastructure is already built, the demand is already coming, and the regulatory framework now allows global Muslim capital to participate.

🏙️ What "Linear Urban Development" Means

Masar Makkah is designed as a 4.8 km pedestrian-friendly corridor — a "path" (مسار) connecting key nodes of the pilgrimage experience. Unlike traditional urban blocks, the linear format creates a continuous journey from transportation hubs through retail, residential, hospitality, and ultimately to Al-Haram. The 3.65km boulevard forms the spine, flanked by towers, plazas, and the King Abdullah Mosque. This urban typology maximises foot traffic, dwell time, and commercial activation along the entire length — similar in concept to Barcelona's La Rambla or Dubai's Dubai Creek Harbour, but at a scale and significance unmatched globally.

📐 Development Model Explained

Structure: The specific opportunity is to develop a branded residence project via a Joint Venture structured inside a fund. The JV will acquire one of the 131 remaining unsold plots — specifically a 4,948 SQM site strategically located in Zone 2, adjacent to the Haramain Train Station, Mall, and King Abdullah Mosque.


WAFI Compliance: All off-plan sales in Saudi Arabia operate under the WAFI regulatory framework (Wafi Programme for Off-Plan Sales), which ties sales collections to construction milestones — protecting buyers and limiting developer risk. This framework provides a SAR 242M+ pre-committed revenue stream before construction completion.


Returns Structure: Returns are realised upon fund termination. The model assumes no dividends during the fund life — all cash flow is reinvested to maximise the final equity return. At the base case (SAR 40.6K/SQM), the project generates SAR 1.95B in gross sales against SAR 650M in development costs, producing a 2.7× cash-on-cash return and 40% IRR. A modest 3% price improvement to SAR 42K/SQM lifts the IRR to 42%.

🌍 Comparable Transactions & Pricing Context

The SAR 40,600/SQM base case pricing for Masar Makkah compares favourably against premium real estate in comparable Islamic destination cities. Properties in central Makkah — particularly those with Haram proximity — are effectively uncapped in their pricing ceiling due to the unique spiritual and regulatory exclusivity. The non-Saudi ownership change means international buyers from Indonesia (270M Muslims), Pakistan (230M), Bangladesh (170M), India (200M+), and Turkey (80M) now represent a new price-competitive demand pool. This structural demand increase, combined with limited supply at the Haram perimeter, creates durable upward pressure on per-SQM values throughout the project lifecycle.
